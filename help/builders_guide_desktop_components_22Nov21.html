1.  Introduction

When building a computer nothing is more frustrating than finding out the motherboard is too large for the case, the processor does not fit into the motherboard, the memory does not fit into the motherboard, the power supply is missing connectors, and the video cable does not connect to the GPU. Selecting compatible computer components is the most challenging aspect of building your own computer. This Web page is a comprehensive review of desktop computer components, including component descriptions, specifications, connections, and compatibility.

2.  Chipset

  • A.k.a., motherboard chipset.
  • A defined set of capabilities upon which a motherboard is built. A motherboard baseline specification. Specifically, a chipset defines: 1.) the processor, memory, expansion cards, data storage devices, and peripheral devices that are compatible with a motherboard; and 2.) the additional devices (e.g., audio and ethernet) and technologies (e.g., RAID and power management) that are integrated into a motherboard. In other words, a chipset defines a motherboard's capabilities, which, in turn, defines a computer's capabilities.
  • Before 2009, consisted of two interconnected chips: 1.) the northbridge chip, which sits between and interfaces with the processor, memory, and GPU (add-in and optional integrated) over high bandwidth buses; and 2.) the southbridge chip, which sits between and interfaces with the computer's additional devices (e.g., internal data storage, USB, optical drives, integrated audio, integrated ethernet, keyboard, mouse, etc.) over lower bandwidth buses.
  • Starting in 2009, consists of one chip: the platform controller hub (PCH), which is essentially a renamed southbridge chip. What happened to the northbridge chip? Starting in 2009, to simplify overall design and improve performance, the northbridge chip memory controller, PCIe port for add-in GPU, and optional integrated GPU were moved onto the processor, and the processor was connected directly to the PCH. This obsoleted the northbridge chip as no longer being needed. Therefore, starting in 2009, memory support, PCIe add-in GPU support, and optional integrated GPU became properties of the processor, not the chipset.
  • Current manufacturers of desktop chipsets are AMD (amd.com) and Intel (intel.com).
  • AMD manufactures AMD desktop chipsets. AMD desktop chipsets support AMD desktop processors. Past manufacturers of AMD desktop chipsets included ATI, NVIDIA, SiS, and VIA. After AMD acquired ATI in 2006, third parties had little future in the desktop chipset market.
  • Intel manufactures Intel desktop chipsets. Intel desktop chipsets support Intel desktop processors. Past manufacturers of Intel desktop chipsets included ATI, NVIDIA, SiS, and VIA.
  • Based on chipset/processor, desktop computers are divided into two types (a.k.a., platforms): 1.) AMD desktop computers, which have an AMD desktop chipset/processor; and 2.) Intel desktop computers, which have an Intel desktop chipset/processor.
  • When building a desktop computer you must decide to build either an AMD desktop computer or an Intel desktop computer.
  • The most important computer component to choose wisely. First choose the chipset that meets your present/future needs, then choose the motherboard.

3.  Motherboard

  • A.k.a., system board, main board, mobo.
  • The main circuit board inside a computer. An implementation of a chipset.
  • Current manufacturers of AMD desktop chipset motherboards include ASRock (asrock.com), ASUS (asus.com), GIGABYTE (gigabyte.com), and MSI (msi.com).
  • Current manufacturers of Intel desktop chipset motherboards include ASRock (asrock.com), ASUS (asus.com), GIGABYTE (gigabyte.com), and MSI (msi.com). Intel stopped manufacturing Intel desktop chipset motherboards in 2013.
  • Form factor: The dimensions, subcomponents, and subcomponent positioning as constitute a general design/form.
  • Motherboard form factor includes, but is not limited to, motherboard size and motherboard/case mount points.
  • Common motherboard form factors: 1.) microATX (μATX), which is 244mm x 244; and 2.) ATX (standard ATX), which is 305mm x 244.
  • Latest microATX specification: Version 1.2 (2004) written by Intel.
  • Latest ATX specification: Version 2.2 (2004) written by Intel.
  • The difference in microATX and ATX size is primarily due to the number of expansion slots.
  • Expansion slot: The connection between expansion (a.k.a., add-in) card and motherboard to install the add-in card.
  • microATX form factor supports up to 4 expansion slots. ATX form factor supports up to 7 expansion slots.
  • Motherboard/case mount points: The connections between motherboard and case to install the motherboard.
  • Case must support motherboard form factor.
  • General price/features: microATX < ATX.
  • Main power connector: The primary connection between power supply and motherboard to power the computer.
  • Latest main power connector type: 24 pin main power connector. Main power connector types oldest to newest: 20 pin main power connector (2000) | 24 pin main power connector (2003).
  • Power supply must provide correct main power connector.
  • Socket: The connection between motherboard and processor to install the processor.
  • Motherboard and processor socket must be the same or compatible.
  • CPU power connector: The connection between power supply and motherboard to power the processor.
  • CPU power connector types: 1.) 4 pin ATX 12V CPU power connector, and 2.) 8 pin EPS 12V CPU power connector (a.k.a., 8 pin ATX 12V CPU power connector).
  • Power supply must provide correct CPU power connector(s).
  • Back USB and audio: The USB ports and audio connectors located on the back of the computer for connecting USB and audio devices. Back USB and audio provided by motherboard back (I/O) panel.
  • Motherboard must support back USB and audio.
  • Front USB and audio: The USB ports and audio connectors located on the front of the computer for connecting USB and audio devices. Front USB provided by connection between motherboard (internal) USB header and case I/O panel. Front audio provided by connection between motherboard (internal) audio header and case I/O panel.
  • Case and motherboard must support front USB and audio.
  • LEDs and switches: The connections between case and motherboard to provide indicator lights and power On/Off and Reset switches.
  • Black wires and white wires are negative. All other wire colors are positive. Polarity (i.e., negative/positive) does not matter for switches, including power On/Off and Reset switches.
  • It is strongly recommended that you read the entire motherboard manual, read all motherboard BIOS release notes, and answer any questions before purchasing a motherboard. After choosing the motherboard, choose a compatible processor.

4.  Processor

  • A.k.a., central processing unit (CPU).
  • Performs the computations/logic/instructions that enable computer functionality.
  • Current manufacturers of desktop processors are AMD (amd.com) and Intel (intel.com).
  • Socket: The connection between motherboard and processor to install the processor.
  • Motherboard and processor socket must be the same or compatible.
  • CPU power connector: The connection between power supply and motherboard to power the processor.
  • CPU power connector types: 1.) 4 pin ATX 12V CPU power connector, and 2.) 8 pin EPS 12V CPU power connector (a.k.a., 8 pin ATX 12V CPU power connector).
  • Power supply must provide correct CPU power connector(s).
  • Boxed processor (except Intel K suffix) is bundled with factory heat sink/fan.
  • Unless overclocking, no fancy processor cooling system required. Get boxed processor and use factory heat sink/fan.
  • Starting in 2009, the motherboard chipset northbridge chip memory controller, PCIe port for add-in GPU, and optional integrated GPU were moved onto the processor. Therefore, starting in 2009, memory support, PCIe add-in GPU support, and optional integrated GPU became properties of the processor, not the motherboard chipset.
  • After choosing the processor, choose compatible memory.

5.  Memory

  • A.k.a., random access memory (RAM), system memory, main memory.
  • Volatile, short-term, fast access data storage for instructions and data needed by the processor.
  • Abbreviations: DDR = Double Data Rate. DDR = DDR1 = DDR 1st generation. DDR2 = DDR 2nd generation. DDR3 = DDR 3rd generation. DDR4 = DDR 4th generation. DDR5 = DDR 5th generation. DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 = DDR and/or DDR2 and/or DDR3 and/or DDR4 and/or DDR5. SDRAM = Synchronous Dynamic Random Access Memory. DIMM = Dual In Line Memory Module = a memory module = a memory stick. Hz = frequency = cycles/second = c/s. T = transfers. XMP = Extreme Memory Profile.
  • Types of memory: DIMM = UDIMM = unbuffered DIMM = unregistered DIMM = desktop memory. DIMM and UDIMM are identical desktop memory that differ only in name, with UDIMM shortened to DIMM for brevity. ECC = error-correcting code = server memory. RDIMM = registered DIMM = buffered DIMM = server memory. DIMMs can be non-ECC or ECC, and unbuffered or registered. To use ECC registered DIMMs the motherboard must support ECC registered DIMMs. Server motherboards, not desktop motherboards, support ECC registered DIMMs. Do not use ECC DIMMs and/or registered DIMMs in desktop computer. Only use non-ECC unbuffered DIMMs (a.k.a, non-ECC UDIMMs) in desktop computer. SODIMM = Small Outline DIMM = laptop/notebook memory = completely incompatible with desktop memory. LP DIMM = low profile DIMM. VLP DIMM = very low profile DIMM. ULP DIMM = ultra low profile DIMM. LP/VLP/ULP DIMMs are DIMMs of decreasing height/increasing cost designed for use in small form factor servers and other devices. To reduce cost, do not use low profile DIMMs for desktop memory.
  • There are only a few memory manufacturers, but many memory vendors. Memory manufacturers develop and produce memory chips (the flat black rectangular boxes) and printed circuit boards (PCBs). Memory vendors assemble the memory chips and PCBs into memory sticks and sell them.
  • Get memory from memory vendors who assemble memory parts from well known memory manufacturers. If the memory vendor does not indicate manufacturer, most motherboard manufacturer memory support lists indicate memory manufacturer in a column usually labeled Chip Brand.
  • Current memory manufacturers include SK hynix (skhynix.com) (formerly Hynix), Micron (micron.com), and Samsung (samsung.com).
  • Current memory vendors include Corsair (corsair.com), Crucial (crucial.com), G.Skill (gskill.com), Kingston (kingston.com), Patriot (patriotmemory.com), PNY (pny.com), TEAMGROUP (teamgroupinc.com) and Thermaltake (thermaltake.com).
  • DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM replaces PC66/100/133 SDRAM.
  • DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM and PC66/100/133 SDRAM are not compatible.
  • DDR SDRAM specifications are created by JEDEC (jedec.org), which requires registration to view the older specifications and a fee to view the newer specifications.
  • Latest memory type: DDR5 SDRAM. Memory types oldest to newest: DDR SDRAM (2002) | DDR2 SDRAM (2004) | DDR3 SDRAM (2007) | DDR4 SDRAM (2014) | DDR5 (2021).
  • DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM are not compatible.
  • DDR SDRAM is 184 pin DIMM, 2.5V, effective frequency 200/266/333/400MHz, and 64MB - 1GB capacity. DDR2 SDRAM is 240 pin DIMM, 1.8V, effective frequency 400/533/666/800/1066MHz, and 256MB - 4GB capacity. DDR3 SDRAM is 240 pin DIMM, 1.5V, effective frequency 800/1066/1333/1600/1866/2133MHz, and 512MB - 8GB capacity. DDR4 SDRAM is 288 pin DIMM, 1.2V, effective frequency 1600/1866/2133/2400/2666/2933/3200MHz, and 2GB - 16GB capacity. DDR5 SDRAM is 288 pin DIMM, 1.1V, effective frequency 4800/5200/5600/6000/6400MHz, and 8GB - 32GB capacity. DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM have different notch position.
  • Many memory vendors produce memory with capacity outside the JEDEC specifications. For example, 32GB DDR4 SDRAM is common. Get memory with capacity outside the JEDEC specifications only if motherboard and processor support it.
  • DDR3L SDRAM is dual voltage DDR3 SDRAM. DDR3L SDRAM is 240 pin DIMM, 1.35V and 1.5V, and effective frequency 800/1066/1333/1600MHz. DDR3/DDR3L SDRAM have identical notch position. DDR3L SDRAM automatically operates at 1.35V or 1.5V depending upon the system. DDR3L SDRAM automatically operates @ 1.35V if all installed memory sticks are DDR3L SDRAM and the processor supports DDR3L SDRAM @ 1.35V; otherwise, the DDR3L SDRAM automatically operates @ 1.5V. In other words, when DDR3L SDRAM is mixed with DDR3 SDRAM and/or the processor does not support DDR3L SDRAM @ 1.35V, the DDR3L SDRAM automatically operates @ 1.5V, not @ 1.35V.
  • PC66/100/133 SDRAM transfer data once per memory I/O pin clock cycle, which is 1transfer/cycle = 1T/c.
  • DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM transfer data twice per memory I/O pin clock cycle, which is 2transfers/cycle = 2T/c.
  • When DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM frequency is discussed the reference is almost always to effective frequency, not memory I/O pin frequency. Effective frequency is the memory I/O pin frequency that would be required to achieve a given transfers/second at 1cycle/transfer, which is T/s x 1c/T. Therefore, for DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM, since data is transferred twice per memory I/O pin cycle, the effective frequency (a.k.a., data frequency) is twice the memory I/O pin frequency.
  • The number of bits that can be transferred over a channel/lane/bus at one time is known as width. The DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM memory bus width is 64 bits. In other words, data is transferred between memory and processor over the memory bus at 64 bits per transfer, which is 64bit/transfer = 64b/T = 8B/T.
  • The peak bandwidth for a single channel of memory is memory I/O pin frequency (cycles/second) x 2transfers/cycle x 8Bytes/transfer. Therefore, for example, since DDR3-1600 has I/O pin frequency 800MHz, the peak bandwidth for a single channel of DDR3-1600 is 800Mc/s x 2T/c x 8B/T = 12800MB/s = 12.8GB/s.
  • Memory is commonly referred to as a memory stick or DIMM. The memory sticks/DIMMs are installed by insertion into motherboard memory stick/DIMM slots (a.k.a., sockets.)
  • To increase the bandwidth between memory and processor multi-channel memory configurations (modes) have been developed. Single channel mode means one 64 bit wide bus between memory and processor is being used. Dual channel mode means two 64 bit wide buses between memory and processor are being used. Triple channel mode means three 64 bit wide buses between memory and processor are being used. Quad channel mode means four 64 bit wide buses between memory and processor are being used. Because channels transmit data simultaneously, increasing channel mode increases bandwidth. For example, DDR3-1600 in single channel mode has peak bandwidth 800Mc/s x 2T/c x 8B/T x 1 channel = 12800MB/s = 12.8GB/s. DDR3-1600 in dual channel mode has peak bandwidth 800Mc/s x 2T/c x 8B/T x 2 channels = 25600MB/s = 25.6GB/s. DDR3-1600 in tri channel mode has peak bandwidth 800Mc/s x 2T/c x 8B/T x 3 channels = 38400MB/s = 38.4GB/s. DDR3-1600 in quad channel mode has peak bandwidth 800Mc/s x 2T/c x 8B/T x 4 channels = 51200MB/s = 51.2GB/s. The motherboard and processor must support the number of channels. Only high end desktop (HEDT) chipsets and processors support triple and quad channel mode.
  • DDR does not mean/imply single channel mode, or vice versa. DDR2 does not mean/imply double channel mode, or vice versa. DDR3 does not mean/imply tri channel mode, or vice versa. DDR4 does not mean/imply quad channel mode, or vice versa.
  • Motherboards and processors that support dual channel mode also support single channel mode. Motherboards and processors that support triple channel mode also support dual and single channel mode. Motherboards and processors that support quad channel mode also support dual and single channel mode and sometimes triple channel mode.
  • Dual, triple, and quad channel mode require proper installation of identical memory sticks; otherwise, the memory subsystem automatically defaults to a lower channel mode.
  • Memory vendors sell packages of two identical memory sticks (a.k.a., kit of 2 x) for dual channel mode, packages of three identical memory sticks (a.k.a., kit of 3 x) for triple channel mode, and packages of four identical memory sticks (a.k.a., kit of 4 x) for quad channel mode.
  • Get kit 2 x for dual channel mode. Get kit 3 x for triple channel mode. Get kit 4 x for quad channel mode.
  • Channels are typically designated by a letter, where first channel is Channel A, 2nd channel is Channel B, etc.
  • Channels typically accept two memory sticks (DIMMs)/channel. DIMM slots (a.k.a., sockets) are typically designated by a number, where each channel's first DIMM slot is DIMM 1 (or DIMM 0) and each channel's second DIMM slot is DIMM 2 (or DIMM 1). Less expensive motherboards may only accept one DIMM/channel.
  • To install kit 2 x in dual channel mode in motherboard with two channels and one DIMM/channel, install first memory stick in Channel A only DIMM slot and install second memory stick in Channel B only DIMM slot. To install kit 2 x in dual channel mode in motherboard with two channels and two DIMMs/channel, install first memory stick in Channel A first DIMM slot and install second memory stick in Channel B first DIMM slot, leaving Channel A second DIMM slot and Channel B second DIMM slot empty for possible future installation of more memory. To install kit 3 x in tri channel mode in motherboard with three channels and two DIMMs/channel, install first memory stick in Channel A first DIMM slot, install second memory stick in Channel B first DIMM slot, and install third memory stick in Channel C first DIMM slot, leaving Channel A second DIMM slot, Channel B second DIMM slot, and Channel C second DIMM slot empty for possible future installation of more memory. To install kit 4 x in quad channel mode in motherboard with four channels and two DIMMs/channel, install first memory stick in Channel A first DIMM slot, install second memory stick in Channel B first DIMM slot, install third memory stick in Channel C first DIMM slot, and install forth memory stick in Channel D first DIMM slot, leaving Channel A second DIMM slot, Channel B second DIMM slot, Channel C second DIMM slot, and Channel D second DIMM slot empty for possible future installation of more memory.
  • Sided. Single sided (SS) means the memory stick has memory chips on one side of the memory stick. Double sided (DS) means the memory sick has chips on both sides of the memory stick. Sidedness and rank are often confused. Single sided does not mean single rank, or vice versa. Double sided does not mean double rank, or vice versa. However, in general, single sided does imply single rank, and vice versa. And, in general, double sided does imply double rank, and vice versa. If necessary, check the memory's specifications for confirmation. Also, in general, sidedness is an older used term and rank is a newer used term. Do not worry about sidedness. If the memory vendor does not indicate sidedness, most motherboard manufacturer memory support lists indicate sidedness in a column usually labeled SS/DS.
  • Rank. The DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM memory bus width is 64 bits. A rank is a set/group (known as block) of memory chips that represents the 64 bits. A single memory chip does not represent the 64 bits. Rather a block of memory chips represents the 64 bits. Single rank means the memory stick has one block/rank of memory chips that represents the 64 bits. Dual rank means the memory stick has a two blocks/ranks of memory chips each of which represents the 64 bits. In dual rank memory, if the two ranks were accessed simultanously, the memory bus width would be 128 bits, which exceeds the JEDEC specifications. Instead, in dual rank memory, each rank is accessed in turn, which keeps the memory bus width at 64 bits per the JEDEC specifications. Single rank is represented as 1R, as in, for example, 1Rx8 and 1Rx16. Dual rank is represented as 2R, as is, for example, 2Rx8 and 2Rx16. Quad (4R) and octa (8R) ranks exist, but are used for special purposes and should not be used in, nor advertised for, desktop computers. The primary purpose of increasing rank is to increase memory stick capacity, not performance. Any performance difference between single and dual rank memory is incidental, depends on the application, and is only a couple of percentage points. Do not worry about rank. If you must worry about rank, get dual rank memory. If the memory vendor does not indicate rank, some motherboard manufacturer memory support lists (e.g., GIGABYTE) indicate rank in a column usually labeled # Of Ranks x DRAM Devices.
  • Bank. The DDR/DDR2/DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM memory bus width is 64 bits. Bank is the width of each memory chip on the memory stick, which is how many bits each memory chip contributes to the 64 bits. Bank is represented as x8 (pronounced "by eight"), as in, for example, 1Rx8 and 1Rx16. x8 memory means each memory chip on the memory stick contributes 8 bits to the 64 bits. x16 memory means each memory chip on the memory stick contributes 16 bits to the 64 bits. Therefore, a 1Rx8 memory stick has 8 memory chips (1 rank = 64bits. 64bits / 8bits/chip = 8 chips). A 1Rx16 memory stick has 4 memory chips (1 rank = 64bits. 64bits / 16bits/chip = 4 chips). A 2Rx8 memory stick has 16 memory chips (2 ranks = 2 x 64bits = 128bits. 128bits / 8bits/chip = 16 chips). A 2Rx16 memory stick has 8 memory chips (2 ranks = 2 x 64bits = 128bits. 128bits / 16bits/chip = 8 chips). x8 memory outperforms x16 memory in virtually all applications due to relatively obscure timing differences inherent to their different memory architectures. In many applications the perfomance improvement is 5-20%, which is significant.
  • Get x8 memory (i.e., 1Rx8 or 2Rx8 memory). Note that memory manufacturers are starting to phase out x8 memory in favor of x16 memory. So, with time, x8 memory is expected to become harder to find. If the memory vendor does not indicate bank, some motherboard manufacturer memory support lists (e.g., GIGABYTE) indicate bank in a column usually labelled # Of Ranks x DRAM Devices.
  • XMP = Extreme Memory Profile. XMP is developed by Intel. Manually overclocking memory is difficult and tedious and involves changing memory frequency, timing, and voltage. XMP makes overclocking memory easy by including user selectable vendor defined overclocking profiles on an XMP memory stick. XMP Certified means the XMP memory passed Intel XMP testing plans with the XMP memory installed on the listed motherboards with the listed processors. XMP Ready means the XMP memory passed vendor XMP testing plans but have not been submitted to or passed Intel XMP testing plans. Latest XMP version: XMP 3.0. XMP versions oldest to newest: XMP 1.0 - 1.3 (DDR3 SDRAM) | XMP 2.0 (DDR4 SDRAM) | XMP 3.0 (DDR5 SDRAM). XMP 2.0 supports up to two vendor profiles. XMP 3.0 supports up to three vendor profiles and up to two user profiles. Using XMP requires XMP memory and a motherboard that supports XMP. Interestingly, although using XMP apparently does not require an unlocked processor, XMP Certified lists include only unlocked processors. The AMD version of XMP is sometimes called various names including AMP (AMD Memory Profile), A-XMP (AMD-XMP), DOCP (Direct Over Clock Profile), and EOCP (Extended Over Clock Profile). Not all DDR3/DDR4/DDR5 SDRAM is XMP. Standard performance/value memory is typically not XMP. High performance/gaming memory is typically XMP. By default, XMP memory operates with JEDEC approved, non-overclocked settings known as fail-safe default boot. This best ensures the XMP memory will function and the computer will boot upon installation of the XMP memory. To actually overclock the XMP memory, boot computer to BIOS/UEFI, select an XMP profile, save the new BIOS/UEFI settings, and reboot the computer. XMP memory adverstised ratings are per an XMP profile's settings, not the default JEDEC fail-safe default boot settings. Although XMP is developed by Intel, XMP is supported by motherboard manufacturers, and there is an XMP certification program, using XMP voids product warrantees. "Altering the frequency and/or voltage outside of Intel specifications may void the processor warranty. Examples: Overclocking and enabling Intel XMP, which is a type of memory overclocking, and using it beyond the given specifications may void the processor warranty." -
  • Intel Extreme Memory Profile (Intel XMP) (intel.com). Therefore, XMP is not recommended.
  • Get standard performance/value memory that is not XMP.
  • Motherboard must support memory type (i.e., DDR SDRAM, DDR2 SDRAM, DDR3 SDRAM, DDR4 SDRAM, or DDR5 SDRAM), memory rank (i.e., 1R or 2R), and memory banks (i.e., x8 or x16). Motherboard and processor must support memory capacity (i.e., size in Bytes) and memory speed (i.e., frequency in Hz). If memory speed exceeds motherboard or processor support, the memory will operate at the maximum speed supported by both the motherboard and processor.
  • Get memory that passed motherboard testing.
  • 32-bit versions of Windows support a maximum of 3.2GB memory, which is accomplished by installing 4GB memory. For greater than 3.2GB memory, install a 64-bit version of Windows.

6.  PCIe

i
For information on add-in GPUs, see Add-In GPU (below).
  • A.k.a., peripheral component interconnect express, PCI Express. PCIE, PCI-E, PCI-e.
  • A serial, scalable, high bandwidth expansion card/expansion slot interface and bus interconnect type used for add-in GPUs, sound, internal data storage, networking, etc.
  • Abbreviations: PCIe Gen1 = PCIe 1st generation = PCIe 1.x = PCIe version 1.0, 1.0a, and 1.1. PCIe Gen2 = PCIe 2nd generation = PCIe 2.x = PCIe version 2.0 and 2.1. PCIe Gen3 = PCIe 3rd generation = PCIe 3.x = PCIe version 3.0 and 3.1. PCIe Gen4 = PCIe 4th generation = PCIe 4.0 = PCIe version 4.0. PCIe 5th generation = PCIe 5.0 = PCIe version 5.0. PCIe = PCIe Gen1 and/or PCIe Gen2 and/or PCIe Gen3 and/or PCIe Gen4 and/or PCIe Gen5.
  • PCIe replaces PCI and AGP.
  • PCIe, PCI, and AGP are not compatible.
  • PCIe specifications are created by PCI Special Interest Group (PCI-SIG) (pcisig.com), which requires a fee to view the specifications.
  • Latest PCIe version: PCIe 5.0. However, as of Oct 2021 there are no PCIe 5.0 implementations. PCIe versions oldest to newest: PCIe 1.0 (2002) | PCIe 1.0a (2003) | PCIe 1.1 (2005) | PCIe 2.0 (2007) | PCIe 2.1 (2009) | PCIe 3.0 (2010) | PCIe 3.1 (2014) | PCIe 4.0 (2017) | PCIe 5.0 (2019).
  • PCIe 1.1, 2.x, 3.x, 4.0, and 5.0 are compatible. In other words, for example, PCIe 1.1, 2.x, 3.x, and 4.0 cards can be installed in PCIe 5.0 slots, and PCIe 5.0 cards can be installed in PCIe 1.1, 2.x, 3.x, and 4.0 slots. Performance, however, is limited to the performance of the slowest device, be it PCIe card or PCIe slot. Therefore, depending upon the usage, the installation might or might not be satisfactory.
  • If the specification for a PCIe card or motherboard PCIe slot does not reference PCIe version, then it is most likely PCIe Gen1.
  • Data is transferred between PCIe card and motherboard PCIe slot over lanes. The number of lanes allowed scales as 1, 2, 4, 8, 12, 16, or 32. The number of lanes present is represented as x1, x2, x4, x8, x12, x16, or x32.
  • A PCIe lane is four PCIe card I/O pins. In each PCIe lane, one pair of I/O pins sends data, the other pair of I/O pins receives data, and data can be transferred in both directions simultaneously. In other words, PCIe data transmission is full duplex at the lane level.
  • PCIe card/slot connector length is directly proportional to the number of lanes present. Therefore, PCIe card/slot connector length: x1 < x2 < x4 < x8 < x12 < x16 < x32.
  • Due to length/cost, PCIe x32 cards/slots are rarely used in personal computers. Therefore, further discussion of PCIe x32 cards/slots is beyond the scope of this page.
  • PCIe transfers data once per PCIe card I/O pin clock cycle, which is 1transfer/cycle = 1T/c.
  • The number of bits that can be transferred over a channel/lane/bus at one time is known as width. PCIe data transfer is serial, which means the PCIe lane width is 1 bit. In other words, data is transferred between PCIe card and PCIe slot/bus at 1 bit per transfer per lane per direction, which is 1bit/transfer per lane per direction = 1b/T per lane per direction.
  • PCIe encoding: PCIe Gen1 and Gen2 data is transferred using the 8b/10b encoding scheme. As a result, each 10 bits of (raw) data transferred contains 8 bits of (usable) original data. In other words, PCIe Gen1 and Gen2 data transfer has 20% overhead ((10-8)/10 x 100 = 20%). PCIe Gen3, Gen4 and Gen5 data is transferred using the 128b/130b encoding scheme. As a result, each 130 bits of (raw) data transferred contains 128 bits of (usable) original data. In other words, PCIe Gen3, Gen4, and Gen5 data transfer has 1.5% overhead ((130-128)/130 x 100 = 1.5%).
  • PCIe Gen1 I/O pin frequency is 2.5GHz, which is 2.5Gcyles/second = 2.5Gc/s.
  • PCIe Gen2 I/O pin frequency is 5.0GHz, which is 5.0Gcyles/second = 5.0Gc/s.
  • PCIe Gen3 I/O pin frequency is 8.0GHz, which is 8.0Gcyles/second = 8.0Gc/s.
  • PCIe Gen4 I/O pin frequency is 16.0GHz, which is 16.0Gcyles/second = 16.0Gc/s.
  • PCIe Gen5 I/O pin frequency is 32.0GHz, which is 32.0Gcyles/second = 32.0Gc/s.
  • PCIe Gen1 I/O pin transfers per second is 2.5Gc/s x 1T/c = 2.5GT/s.
  • PCIe Gen2 I/O pin transfers per second is 5.0Gc/s x 1T/c = 5.0GT/s.
  • PCIe Gen3 I/O pin transfers per second is 8.0Gc/s x 1T/c = 8.0GT/s.
  • PCIe Gen4 I/O pin transfers per second is 16.0Gc/s x 1T/c = 16.0GT/s.
  • PCIe Gen5 I/O pin transfers per second is 32.0Gc/s x 1T/c = 32.0GT/s.
  • PCIe Gen1 bandwidth per lane per direction (raw) is 2.5Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T = 2.5Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen2 bandwidth per lane per direction (raw) is 5.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T = 5.0Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen3 bandwidth per lane per direction (raw) is 8.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T = 8.0Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen4 bandwidth per lane per direction (raw) is 16.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T = 16.0Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen5 bandwidth per lane per direction (raw) is 32.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T = 32.0Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen1 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) is 2.5Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) = 2.0Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen2 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) is 5.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) = 4.0Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen3 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) is 8.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) = 7.9Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen4 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) is 16.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) = 15.8Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen5 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) is 32.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) = 31.5Gb/s.
  • PCIe Gen1 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) in Bytes/s is 2.5Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) x 1B/8b = 250MB/s.
  • PCIe Gen2 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) in Bytes/s is 5.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) x 1B/8b = 500MB/s.
  • PCIe Gen3 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) in Bytes/s is 8.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) x 1B/8b = 985MB/s.
  • PCIe Gen4 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) in Bytes/s is 16.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) x 1B/8b = 1969MB/s.
  • PCIe Gen5 bandwidth per lane per direction (usable) in Bytes/s is 32.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) x 1B/8b = 3938MB/s.
  • PCIe Gen1 x16 bandwidth bidirectional (usable) in Bytes/s is 2.5Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 16 lanes x 2 directions x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) x 1B/8b = 8.0GB/s.
  • PCIe Gen2 x16 bandwidth bidirectional (usable) in Bytes/s is 5.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 16 lanes x 2 directions x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) x 1B/8b = 16.0GB/s.
  • PCIe Gen3 x16 bandwidth bidirectional (usable) in Bytes/s is 8.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 16 lanes x 2 directions x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) x 1B/8b = 31.5GB/s.
  • PCIe Gen4 x16 bandwidth bidirectional (usable) in Bytes/s is 16.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 16 lanes x 2 directions x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) x 1B/8b = 63.0GB/s.
  • PCIe Gen5 x16 bandwidth bidirectional (usable) in Bytes/s is 32.0Gc/s x 1T/c x 1b/T x 16 lanes x 2 directions x 128b(usable)/130b(raw) x 1B/8b = 126.0GB/s.
  • PCIe Gen5 delivers twice the bandwidth per lane compared to PCIe Gen4, PCIe Gen4 delivers twice the bandwidth per lane compared to PCIe Gen3, PCIe Gen3 delivers twice the bandwidth per lane compared to PCIe Gen2, and PCIe Gen2 delivers twice the bandwidth per lane compared to PCIe Gen1. Therefore, for example, PCIe Gen5 x16 cards in PCIe Gen5 x16 slots deliver twice the bandwidth of PCIe Gen4 x16 cards in PCIe Gen4 x16 slots, PCIe Gen4 x16 cards in PCIe Gen4 x16 slots deliver twice the bandwidth of PCIe Gen3 x16 cards in PCIe Gen3 x16 slots, PCIe Gen3 x16 cards in PCIe Gen3 x16 slots deliver twice the bandwidth of PCIe Gen2 x16 cards in PCIe Gen2 x16 slots, and PCIe Gen2 x16 cards in PCIe Gen2 x16 slots deliver twice the bandwidth of PCIe Gen1 x16 cards in PCIe Gen1 x16 slots.
  • Since PCIe 2.0 and 2.1 are identical in terms of bandwidth, the only reason to get a PCIe 2.1 card instead of a PCIe 2.0 card is for non-bandwidth related PCIe 3.0 compatibility enhancements.
  • Since PCIe 3.0 and 3.1 are identical in terms of bandwidth, the only reason to get a PCIe 3.1 card instead of a PCIe 3.0 card is for non-bandwidth related PCIe 3.1 enhancements.
  • To reduce cost, PCIe cards are manufactured with only the number of lanes needed to support the device bandwidth requirement. For example, a basic gigabit network adapter (a.k.a., network interface card) need only be x1.
  • PCIe cards can be installed in PCIe slots having an equal or greater number of lanes. For example, a PCIe x1 card can be installed in a PCIe x1, x2, x4, x8, x12, or x16 slot.
  • Ideally, since PCIe cards can be installed in PCIe slots having an equal or a greater number of lanes, all PCIe slots would be x16. To reduce cost, motherboards are manufactured with PCIe x1, x2, x4, x8, and x12 slots, rather than all x16 slots.
  • To reduce cost, some PCIe slots have more lanes physically than electrically. For example, a common PCIe slot is physically x16, but electrically x4, which is known as x16 (x4 mode). A PCIe x16 (x4 mode) slot accepts PCIe cards up to x16 in length, but only the first four lanes are functional. In other words, PCIe x8, x12, and x16 cards can be installed in a PCIe x16 (x4 mode) slot, but the bandwidth is limited to four lanes. Therefore, depending upon the usage, the installation might or might not be satisfactory. Although PCIe slots with more lanes physically than electrically might seem odd, they provide functionality and flexibility that would be lost if the slot was the same length physically as electrically.
  • PCIe slots provide up to 75W to PCIe cards. Therefore, PCIe cards that use less than 75W might have a power supply minimum wattage requirement, and PCIe cards that use more than 75W always have a power supply minimum wattage requirement and one or more PCIe power connectors.
  • PCIe power connector: The connection between power supply and PCIe card to provide additional power to the PCIe card.
  • PCIe power connector types: 1.) 6 pin PCIe power connector, and 2.) 8 pin PCIe power connector.
  • To install PCIe card, power supply must provided PCIe card power supply requirements, if any, including power supply minimum wattage and correct PCIe power connector(s).
  • Some PCIe cards are very long and cannot be installed in smaller cases. In addition, although PCIe cards install in a single PCIe slot, some PCIe cards are very wide (a.k.a., double slot) and can obstruct adjacent motherboard expansion slots and/or have connectors that occupy two adjacent case expansion slots.
  • Case must support PCIe card length. In addition, adjacent motherboard and case expansions slots must support PCIe card width.

7.  GPU

  • A.k.a., graphics processing unit.
  • Performs the graphics computations/logic/instructions. Output is displayed on the monitor.
  • GPU types: integrated GPUs and add-in GPUs.

7.1.  Integrated GPU

  • A.k.a., iGPU, integrated graphics processor (IGP), integrated graphics, built-in graphics, on-board graphics, on-chip graphics.
  • A GPU built into (i.e. integrated into) another computer component.
  • Before 2009, integrated GPUs were built into the motherboard chipset northbridge chip; however, not all motherboard chipset northbridge chips before 2009 included an integrated GPU.
  • Starting in 2009, integrated GPU are built into the processor; however, not all processors starting in 2009 include an integrated GPU.
  • An optional component. Therefore, to use integrated GPU, make sure motherboard (before 2009) or processor (starting in 2009) has integrated GPU. If integrated GPU is not present, not satisfactory, or not working, install add-in GPU.
  • Integrated GPU manufacturers include AMD (amd.com) and Intel (intel.com).
  • Just like a processor (CPU) works on bits in memory (system memory), a GPU works on bits in graphics memory. Integrated GPUs do not have their own built-in graphics memory. Instead, integrated GPUs use a portion of the computer system memory as their graphics memory. Therefore, if you want to use integrated GPU and intend to run graphics or memory intensive applications, it is recommended to get more, not less, system memory.
  • Motherboard support for processor integrated GPU is simply providing; 1.) an interface to the processor integrated GPU, and 2.) video connector(s).
  • Video cable: The connection between monitor video connector and integrated GPU video connector to transfer the display signal.
  • Latest video connector type: DisplayPort. Video connector types oldest to newest: D-Sub (VGA) | DVI | HDMI | DisplayPort.
  • Video cable must provide correct monitor video connector and correct integrated GPU video connector.

  • PCIe slots provide up to 75W to PCIe cards. Therefore, PCIe add-in GPUs that use less than 75W might have a power supply minimum wattage requirement, and PCIe add-in GPUs that use more than 75W always have a power supply minimum wattage requirement and one or more PCIe power connectors.
  • PCIe power connector: The connection between power supply and PCIe add-in GPU to provide additional power to the PCIe add-in GPU.
  • PCIe power connector types: 1.) 6 pin PCIe power connector, and 2.) 8 pin PCIe power connector.
  • To install PCIe add-in GPU, power supply must provided PCIe add-in GPU power supply requirements, if any, including power supply minimum wattage and correct PCIe power connector(s).

  • Some PCIe add-in GPUs are very long and cannot be installed in smaller cases. In addition, although PCIe add-in GPUs install in a single PCIe slot, some PCIe add-in GPUs are very wide (a.k.a., double slot) and can obstruct adjacent motherboard expansion slots and/or have connectors that occupy two adjacent case expansion slots.
  • Case must support PCIe add-in GPU length. In addition, adjacent motherboard and case expansions slots must support PCIe add-in GPU width.
  • Video cable: The connection between monitor video connector and add-in GPU video connector to transfer the display signal.
  • Latest video connector type: DisplayPort. Video connector types oldest to newest: D-Sub (VGA) | DVI | HDMI | DisplayPort.
  • Video cable must provide correct monitor video connector and correct add-in GPU video connector.

8.  Internal Data Storage

  • Non-volatile, long-term data storage for operating system files, application files, and data files which resides internal to (i.e., inside) the case.
  • Latest internal data storage type: solid state drive (SSD). Internal data storage types oldest to newest: hard drive (HD) (1986) | solid state drive (SSD) (2007).
  • Internal data storage technology types: 1.) HD: electromechanical. 2.) SSD: solid state.
  • Bits stored: 1.) HD: magneticallly on spinning platters. 2.) SSD: electrically in transistors.
  • Internal data storage interface types: 1.) PATA: PATA HDs. 2.) SATA: SATA HDs and 2.5" SATA SSDs. 3.) PCIe: M.2 NVMe PCIe x4 SSDs.
  • Motherboard must support internal data storage interface.

8.1.  HD

  • A.k.a., hard drive, hard disk, hard disk drive (HDD).
  • Benefits of HDs compared to SSDs: 1.) High capacity drives are less expensive. 2.) When they fail, they tend to fail with warning and some data recovery may be possible. 3.) More reliable for long-term offline (unplugged) data archiving.
  • Drawbacks of HDs compared to SSDs: 1.) Slower: operating system and applications load into memory slower. 2.) Moving parts: less shock/vibration resistant, require more power, generate more heat, noisier. 3.) Larger and weight considerably more. 4.) Need to periodically defragment drive. Overall, when building desktop computer with HDs for internal storage, because HDs require more power and generate more heat, get slightly higher wattage power supply, get slightly larger case, and get more chassis fans, all of which increase cost.

8.1.1.  PATA HD

i
Prior to the release of SATA HDs, PATA HDs were primarily referred to as ATA/IDE HDs.
  • A.k.a., PATA hard drive, PATA hard disk drive (PATA HDD), parallel AT attachment, parallel ATA, ATA, IDE, EIDE, Ultra DMA, hard drive, hard disk, hard disk drive.
  • 3.5" electromechanical internal data storage device with movable read/write heads that retrieve/store data magnetically on spinning platters and is connected to motherboard via PATA interface.
  • PATA HDs replaced by SATA HDs. PATA HDs are considered legacy devices. New motherboards do not support PATA HDs.
  • PATA HDs and SATA HDs are not compatible.
  • Latest PATA version: PATA/133. PATA versions oldest to newest: PATA/33 (33MB/s) (1998) | PATA/66 (66MB/s) (2000) | PATA/100 (100MB/s) (2002) | PATA/133 (133MB/s) (2004).
  • PATA/33/66/100/133 are compatible. In other words, for example, PATA/33 HDs can be installed on PATA/133 channels, and PATA/133 HDs can be installed on PATA/33 channels. Performance, however, is limited to the performance of the slowest device, be it PATA HDs or PATA channel.
  • PATA HD data cable: The connection between PATA HD and PATA channel to transfer data.
  • PATA HD data cable types: 1.) 40 pin 40 wire/conductor data cable, which is compatible with PATA HD operation at 33MB/s, not 66/100/133MB/s; and 2.) 40 pin 80 wire/conductor data cable, which is compatible with PATA HD operation at 33/66/100/133MB/s, and is required for PATA HD operation at 66/100/133MB/s.
  • Two PATA HDs in master/slave configuration can be installed per PATA HD data cable/channel.
  • PATA HD jumper: A mechanism for setting the PATA HD drive position on the PATA HD data cable/channel. Possible settings: master (MA), slave (SL), and cable select (CS).
  • PATA HD data cable connector relative positions: motherboard (MB) (blue), slave (SL) (gray), and master (MA) (black): MB--------------------SL-----MA.
  • Peripheral power connector: The connection between power supply and PATA HD to power the PATA HD.
  • Peripheral power connector type: 4 pin peripheral power connector (a.k.a., 4 pin molex power connector).
  • To install PATA HD, power supply must provide peripheral power connector.
  • Access time (in milliseconds) = seek time (function of read/write head movement measured in milliseconds) + latency (function of platter rpm measured in milliseconds).

8.1.2.  SATA HD

i
With the release of SATA HDs, HD data transfer rates started to be reported in bits/second, not Bytes/second as was the convention for PATA HDs. It is no accident that this change in convention helped hide the fact that the initial SATA HDs had data transfer rates only slightly faster than the latest PATA HDs.
  • A.k.a., SATA hard drive, SATA hard disk drive (SATA HDD), serial AT attachment, serial ATA, hard drive, hard disk, hard disk drive.
  • 3.5" electromechanical internal data storage device with movable read/write heads that retrieve/store data magnetically on spinning platters and is connected to motherboard via SATA interface.
  • SATA ports are provided by SATA host controllers. SATA host controllers can be integrated into motherboard chipsets, added to motherboards by motherboard manufacturers, or installed via SATA host controller expansion cards (a.k.a., SATA host adapters). If Windows does not provide a driver for the SATA host controller, then the user must install the Windows driver manually. Windows Setup for Windows XP and earlier do not provide SATA host controller driver. During the beginning of Windows Setup for Windows XP and earlier, press F6, insert disk with SATA host controller driver, and select the driver.
  • SATA HD manufacturers include Hitachi (hitachi.com), Seagate (seagate.com), which acquired Maxtor in 2005, and Western Digital (wdc.com).
  • SATA HDs replace PATA HDs.
  • SATA HDs and PATA HDs are not compatible.
  • SATA specifications are created by SATA-IO (sata-io.org), which requires a fee to view the specifications.
  • Latest SATA version: SATA 6.0Gb/s. SATA versions oldest to newest: SATA 1.5Gb/s (SATA Revision 1.0, SATA150, SATA I) (2001) | SATA 3.0Gb/s (SATA Revision 2.0, SATA300, SATA II) (2005) | SATA 6.0Gb/s (SATA Revision 3.0, SATA600, SATA III) (2009).
  • SATA 1.5Gb/s, 3.0Gb/s, and 6.0Gb/s are compatible. In other words, for example, SATA 1.5Gb/s HDs can be installed on SATA 6.0Gb/s ports, and SATA 6.0Gb/s HDs can be installed on SATA 1.5Gb/s ports. Performance, however, is limited to the performance of the slowest device, be it SATA HD or SATA port.
  • SATA HD data is transferred using the 8b/10b encoding scheme. As a result, each 10 bits of (raw) data transferred contains 8 bits of (usable) original data. In other words, SATA HD data transfer has 20% overhead.
  • SATA 1.5Gb/s bandwidth (usable) in Bytes/s is 1.5Gb/s x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) x 1B/8b = 150MB/s.
  • SATA 3.0Gb/s bandwidth (usable) in Bytes/s is 3.0Gb/s x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) x 1B/8b = 300MB/s.
  • SATA 6.0Gb/s bandwidth (usable) in Bytes/s is 6.0Gb/s x 8b(usable)/10b(raw) x 1B/8b = 600MB/s.
  • SATA HD jumper: A mechanism for setting the SATA HD data transfer rate. Enables newer SATA HDs to function with older SATA host controllers that do not comply with newer SATA specifications by forcing the SATA HD to operate at an older/lower SATA data transfer rate. In theory, SATA 1.5Gb/s, 3.0Gb/s, and 6.0Gb/s compatibility means SATA HD jumpering should not be necessary. In practice, however, since not all older SATA host controllers comply with newer SATA specifications, SATA HD jumpering is necessary. To ensure SATA HD function, jumper the SATA hard drive to its lowest data transfer rate, which is typically the default/factory setting. If the SATA host controller's highest data transfer rate is equal to the SATA HD's lowest data transfer rate, then jumpering the SATA HD to its lowest data transfer rate does not minimize SATA hard drive performance. If the SATA host controller's highest data transfer rate is equal to or greater than the SATA HD's highest data transfer rate, then jumper the SATA HD jumper to its highest data transfer rate to maximize SATA HD performance. If the SATA HDs does not function when jumpered to its highest data transfer rate, then the SATA HD is likely a newer SATA version than the SATA host controller, and jumper the SATA HD to its lowest data transfer rate to enable function.
  • SATA HD data cable: The connection between SATA HD and SATA port to transfer data.
  • SATA HD data cable types: 1.) SATA Revision 1.0 and 2.0 data cables, which are compatible with SATA HD operation at 1.5Gb/s and 3.0Gb/s, not 6.0Gb/s; and 2.) SATA Revision 2.6 and 3.0 data cables, which are compatible with SATA HD operation at 1.5Gb/s, 3.0Gb/s, and 6.0Gb/s, and are required for SATA HD operation at 6.0Gb/s. SATA Revision 1.0, 2.0, 2.6, and 3.0 data cable connectors are 7 pins, have identical shape, and either end of the cable can be connected to the SATA HD or SATA port. SATA Revision 2.6 and 3.0 data cables differ from SATA Revision 1.0 and 2.0 data cables in that SATA Revision 2.6 and 3.0 data cables are of higher quality and are shielded. Use SATA Revision 2.6 and 3.0 data cables, not SATA Revision 1.0 and 2.0 data cables, in a SATA 6.0Gb/s environment. Using SATA Revision 1.0 and 2.0 data cables in a SATA 6.0Gb/s environment can lead to data integrity and performance issues.
  • SATA HD data cable lengths: Minimum is 300mm (12 inches). Maximum is 1.0 meter (39 inches). The shorter the better, but not shorter than the minimum.
  • Only one SATA HD can be installed per SATA data cable/port.
  • SATA power connector: The connection between power supply and SATA HD to power the SATA HD.
  • SATA power connector type: 15 pin SATA power connector.
  • To install SATA HD, power supply must provide SATA power connector.
  • Access time (in milliseconds) = seek time (function of read/write head movement measured in milliseconds) + latency (function of platter rpm measured in milliseconds).
  • Performance: SATA HDs do not perform anywhere near the SATA rated speed. Typical 6.0Gb/s SATA HDs operate at ~170MB/s read and ~150MB/s write, which is less than one third the SATA rated speed.

8.2.  SSD

i
Numerous types of SSDs have been used for internal data storage. The following SSD types are not covered as being superseded, too expensive, and/or not widely adopted: mini-SATA (mSATA) SSD, half height, half length (HHHL) PCIe SSD add-in-card (AiC), and U.2 NVMe SSD.
  • A.k.a., solid state drive, flash drive, flash memory drive.
  • Benefits of SSDs compared to HDs: 1.) Faster: operating system and applications load into memory faster. 2.) No moving parts: more shock/vibration resistant, require less power, generate less heat, quieter. 3.) Smaller and weight considerably less. 4.) No need to defragment drive. Overall, when building desktop computer with SSDs for internal storage, because SSDs require less power and generate less heat, can get slightly lower wattage power supply, can get slightly smaller case, and can get less chassis fans, all of which decrease cost.
  • Drawbacks of SSDs compared to HDs: 1.) High capacity drives are more expensive. 2.) Although less a concern with newer SSDs, the data storage transistors can only be written to a finite number of times. SSD endurance (lifespan) is expressed in terms of terabytes written (TBW), program/erase (P/E) cycles, and mean time between failures (MTBF). To increase endurance, SSDs employ techniques to avoiding writing to the same transistors too many times. If two SSDs are identical or nearly identical except for endurance, get SSD with greater endurance. Lastly, increased SSD free space increases SSD endurance. Therefore, if intend to frequently download, move, create, and/or edit large files, and/or if expect SSD free space to vary greatly, it is recommended to get slightly higher, not lower, capacity SSD. 3.) When they fail, they tend to fail without warning and data recovery is virtually impossible. 4.) Less reliable for long-term offline (unplugged) data archiving.

8.2.1.  2.5" SATA SSD

  • Initially used for mobile internal data storage (1991). Later adopted for desktop internal data storage.
  • All desktop computers using SATA HD for internal data storage can be upgraded to use 2.5" SATA SSD for internal data storage.
  • Near drop in replacement for SATA HD.
  • Uses same data cable and power connector as SATA HD.
  • Some cases provide 2.5" SATA SSD bays. If case does not provide 2.5" SATA SSD bays, get 3.5" SATA HD bay to 2.5" SATA SSD bay adapter.
  • After installing 2.5" SATA SSD, boot computer to BIOS/UEFI and make sure 2.5" SATA SSD is selected as boot drive. Also, for best SATA SSD performance, set BIOS/UEFI SATA mode to AHCI, not IDE nor RAID.
    i
    In some BIOSes/UEFIs AHCI is referred to as SATA, and IDE is referred to as Legacy or ATA.
    !
    Changing BIOS/UEFI SATA mode from IDE or RAID to AHCI likely requires reinstalling Windows.
  • If motherboard supports 6.0Gb/s SATA, get 6.0Gb/s 2.5" SATA SSD, not 3.0Gb/s 2.5" SATA SSD, and connect 2.5" SATA SSD to motherboard 6.0Gb/s SATA port.
  • Most popular flash memory type: 3D NAND (2013).
  • Performance: 2.5" SATA SSDs perform close to the SATA rated speed. Typical 6.0Gb/s 2.5" SATA SSDs operate at ~525MB/s read and ~475MB/s write, which is close to the SATA rated speed. 6.0Gb/s 2.5" SATA SDDs are ~3x faster than 6.0Gb/s SATA HDs.

9.  USB

  • A.k.a., universal serial bus.
  • A receptacle/plug interface and bus interconnect type well suited for connecting peripheral devices to computers.
  • A USB is created when a USB device is connected to a USB host. A USB device is a peripheral device with a USB plug. USB plugs can be built into peripheral devices and/or provided by an attachment, such as a USB cable. Common USB device types include keyboards, mice, external data storage devices, printers, network adapters, and digital cameras and camcorders. A USB host is a computer with one or more USB ports. USB ports are provided by USB host controllers. USB host controllers can be integrated into motherboard chipsets, added to motherboards by motherboard manufacturers, or installed via USB host controller expansion cards (a.k.a., USB host adapters). If Windows does not provide a driver for the USB device or USB host controller, then the user must install the driver manually.
  • Abbreviations: USB 1.x = USB 1.0 and USB 1.1.
  • Latest USB version: USB 3.0. USB versions oldest to newest: USB 1.0 (1996) | USB 1.1 (1998) | USB 2.0 (Hi-Speed USB) (2000) | USB 3.0 (SuperSpeed USB) (2008).
  • USB 1.x, 2.0 and 3.0 are compatible. In other words, for example, USB 1.x devices can be installed on USB 3.0 ports, and USB 3.0 devices can be installed on USB 1.x ports. Performance, however, is limited to the performance of the slowest device, be it USB device, USB port, or USB cable.
  • USB 1.x defines 4 pin Standard-A connectors as USB ports/plugs and data transfer rates at 1.5Mb/s (Low-Speed USB) and 12Mb/s (Full-Speed USB).
  • USB 2.0 defines 4 pin Standard-A connectors as USB ports/plugs and data transfer rates at 1.5Mb/s (Low-Speed USB), 12Mb/s (Full-Speed USB), and 480Mb/s (Hi-Speed USB). In terms of connectors and data transfer rates, USB 2.0 is identical to USB 1.x with the exception of an additional data transfer rate at 480Mb/s (Hi-Speed USB).
  • USB 3.0 defines 9 pin Standard-A connectors as USB ports/plugs and data transfer rates at 1.5Mb/s (Low-Speed USB), 12Mb/s (Full-Speed USB), 480Mb/s (Hi-Speed USB), and 5.0Gb/s (SuperSpeed USB). In terms of connectors and data transfer rates, USB 3.0 is identical to USB 2.0 with the exception of 5 additional pins, which are used exclusively for transferring data at 5.0Gb/s (i.e. SuperSpeed USB). In other words, USB 3.0 consists of two distinct buses; 1.) a 4 pin USB 2.0, and 2.) a 5 pin SuperSpeed USB.
  • Common USB port types: 1.) USB 2.0 port, which is compatible with USB 1.x, 2.0, and 3.0 plugs and data transfer rates at 1.5Mb/s (Low-Speed USB), 12Mb/s (Full-Speed USB), and 480Mb/s (Hi-Speed USB), not 5.0Gb/s (SuperSpeed USB); and 2.) USB 3.0 port, which is compatible with USB 1.x, 2.0, and 3.0 plugs and data transfer rates at 1.5Mb/s (Low-Speed USB), 12Mb/s (Full-Speed USB), 480Mb/s (Hi-Speed USB), and 5.0Gb/s (SuperSpeed USB).
  • For SuperSpeed USB (i.e., USB 3.0 data transfer rates at 5.0Gb/s), get USB 3.0 devices, a motherboard with integrated USB 3.0 host controllers, install USB 3.0 drivers, if necessary, and use USB 3.0 cables, not USB 1.x or 2.0 cables, if necessary.
  • Back USB ports: The USB ports located on the back of the computer for connecting USB devices. Back USB ports provided by motherboard back (I/O) panel.
  • Motherboard must support back USB.
  • Front USB ports: The USB ports located on the front of the computer for connecting USB devices. Front USB provided by connection between motherboard (internal) USB header and case I/O panel.
  • Case and motherboard must support front USB.
  • USB 3.0 recommends blue USB 3.0 connectors to distinguish them from USB 1.x and 2.0 connectors.

10.  Case

  • A.k.a., chassis, enclosure.
  • The computer housing and component attachment system.
  • Motherboard form factor includes, but is not limited to, motherboard size and motherboard/case mount points.
  • Common motherboard form factors: 1.) microATX (μATX), which is 244mm x 244; and 2.) ATX (standard ATX), which is 305mm x 244.
  • The difference in microATX and ATX size is primarily due to the number of expansion slots.
  • Expansion slot (motherboard definition): The connection between expansion (a.k.a., add-in) card and motherboard to install the add-in card.
  • Expansion slot (case definition): The area at the back of the case to access the expansion (a.k.a., add-in) card connectors.
  • microATX form factor supports up to 4 expansion slots. ATX form factor supports up to 7 expansion slots. Therefore, microATX compatible case should have at least 4 expansions slots, and ATX compatible case should have at least 7 expansion slots.
  • Motherboard/case mount points: The connections between motherboard and case to install the motherboard.
  • microATX and ATX form factors have a number of identical motherboard/case mount point positions. Therefore, cases that support ATX motherboards also support microATX motherboards. However, cases that support microATX motherboards might not support ATX motherboards.
  • Case must support motherboard form factor.
  • Common case types: mini tower, mid tower, and full tower.
  • Typical case dimensions and motherboard form factor support: Mini towers are typically 350-425mm x 175-200 x 425-475 (H x W x L). Mini towers typically support microATX motherboards. Larger mini towers might also support ATX motherboards. Mid towers are typically 425-550mm x 175-220 x 450-550 (H x W x L). Mid towers typically support microATX and ATX motherboards. Full towers are typically 550mm+ x 200+ x 550+ (H x W x L). Full towers typically support microATX and ATX motherboards. Case dimension standards, however, do not exist. Therefore, for example, Case X might be one manufacturers mini tower and another manufacturers mid tower. Moreover, for example, Manufacturer A might have some mini towers that support only microATX motherboards, and other mini towers that support both microATX and ATX motherboards.
  • It is strongly recommended that you check case specifications for dimensions and motherboard form factor support before purchasing a case.
  • Recommended case sizes for microATX and ATX motherboards: microATX motherboard is 244mm x 244. ATX motherboard is 305mm x 244. Typical power supply height is 86mm. Typical CD/DVD burner (not Blu-ray) depth/length is 170mm. Typical PATA/SATA hard drive depth/length is 147mm. Therefore, for microATX motherboards, ~350mm x 425 (H x L) is the absolute minimum case size, 365mm x 450 (H x L) is a practical minimum case size, and 400mm x 475 (H x L) is a preferred minimum case size, the latter being the minimum for users who frequently open the case. And for ATX motherboards, ~410mm x 425 (H x L) is the absolute minimum case size, 425mm x 450 (H x L) is a practical minimum case size, and 450mm x 475 (H x L) is a preferred minimum case size, the latter being the minimum for users who frequently open the case.
  • Bays: The place to install: 1.) optical drives, which are typically 5.25" wide; 2.) PATA and SATA hard drives, which are typically 3.5" wide; 3.) solid state drives, which are typically 2.5" wide, and 4.) floppy disk drives, which are typically 2.5" wide.
  • Bay types: 1.) external bays, which are accessible from outside the case and, therefore, are commonly used for removable media devices such as optical drives and floppy disk drives; and 2.) internal bays, which are only accessible from inside the case and, therefore, are commonly used for internal data storage devices such as PATA and SATA hard drives and solid state drives.
  • Bays misc: Brackets (a.k.a., adapters) convert larger bays to smaller bays. Drives with spinning components are best installed with the spinning components oriented horizontally (flat), not vertically (on edge). Some bays are tool-free, which means the drives, unadulterated, are slid into the bays and secured by a pinch/grasp mechanism. Some bays employ a drive caddy, which means a device is attached to the drives that allows them to be slid into reciprocating bays and secured by a snap-in mechanism. In general, although some designs are better than others, I prefer drive caddies over tool-free.
  • Case must support PCIe card/add-in GPU length. In addition, adjacent motherboard and case expansions slots must support PCIe card/add-in GPU width.
  • Front USB and audio: The USB ports and audio connectors located on the front of the computer for connecting USB and audio devices. Front USB provided by connection between motherboard (internal) USB header and case I/O panel. Front audio provided by connection between motherboard (internal) audio header and case I/O panel.
  • Case and motherboard must support front USB and audio.
  • Side panels that swing open and do not detach can be a nuisance when working inside the computer.
  • LEDs and switches: The connections between case and motherboard to provide indicator lights and power On/Off and Reset switches.
  • Black wires and white wires are negative. All other wire colors are positive. Polarity (i.e., negative/positive) does not matter for switches, including power On/Off and Reset switches.
  • Case and power supply design affect computer cooling. Manufacturers of both cases and power supplies should be best positioned and most motivated to develop solutions to computer cooling problems. Therefore, I prefer cases from manufactures of both cases and power supplies.
  • Manufacturers of both cases and power supplies include Antec (antec.com), Cooler Master (coolermaster.com), Corsair (corsair.com), Lian Li (lian-li.com), and Thermaltake (thermaltakeusa.com).
  • Unless overclocking and/or case interior is unusually segmented, no fancy case cooling system required and one case mounted fan (back, top, or front) is sufficient.
  • Case style: The appearance of the case.
  • Case style types: 1.) business, which do not have lights, see through side panels, bright colors, and/or odd shapes; and 2.) enthusiast, which have lights, see through side panels, bright colors, and/or odd shapes.
  • Case style types by manufacturer from more business to enthusiast oriented: Lian Li | Antec | Corsair | Cooler Master | Thermaltake. Be careful purchasing a case without seeing it plugged in or you might get a Christmas tree.
  • Look for case/power supply bundle to reduce cost. Be careful, however, that case/power supply bundles - even from name brand manufacturers - often include small, not large, mini towers, mid towers, and full towers.

11.  Power Supply

  • A.k.a., power supply unit (PSU).
  • Distributes electricity to computer components.
  • Abbreviations: ATX12V v1.x = ATX12V version 1.0, 1.1, 1.2, and 1.3. ATX12V v2.x = ATX12V version 2.0, 2.01, 2.1, 2.2, 2.3, and 2.31.
  • Latest power supply version: ATX12V v2.31. Power supply versions oldest to newest: ATX12V v1.0 (2000) | ATX12V v1.1 (2000) | ATX12V v1.2 (2002) | ATX12V v1.3 (2003) | ATX12V v2.0 (2003) | ATX12V v2.01 (2004) | ATX12V v2.1 (2005) | ATX12V v2.2 (2005) | ATX12V v2.3 (2007).
  • ATX12V v2.x dimensions: 150mm x 140mm x 86mm (W x L x H).
  • For current microATX and ATX form factor motherboards, get power supply that is ATX12V v2.2 compliant or above and provides all required connectors.
  • Power supply must support motherboard form factor.
  • Do not modify connectors. Do not use mismatched connectors. Do not use adapters. Instead, get a power supply that provides all required connectors.
  • To determine power supply wattage requirement, use power supply calculator and then add 100W.
    i
    For power supply calculators, see Additional Reading (below).
  • Top/bottom fan and installation orientation: Higher quality power supplies include a top or bottom fan in addition to the rear fan. If power supply includes a top or bottom fan, then install power supply right-side-up or up-side-down as necessary so that top or bottom fan airflow is not impeded.
  • Main power connector: The primary connection between power supply and motherboard to power the computer.
  • Latest main power connector type: 24 pin main power connector. Main power connector types oldest to newest: 20 pin main power connector (2000) | 24 pin main power connector (2003). ATX12V v1.x describes a 20 pin (2 rows x 10 pin each) main power connector. To support the PCIe 75W power requirement, ATX12V v2.x describes a 24 pin (2 rows x 12 pin each) main power connector. Except for the 4 additional pins, the 20 pin and 24 pin main power connectors are identical. Therefore, for compatibility with old and new motherboards, many power supplies provide a 20+4 pin main power connector.
  • Power supply must provide correct main power connector.
  • CPU power connector: The connection between power supply and motherboard to power the processor.
  • CPU power connector types: 1.) 4 pin (2 rows x 2 pins each) ATX 12V CPU power connector, and 2.) 8 pin (2 rows x 4 pins each) EPS 12V CPU power connector (a.k.a., 8 pin ATX 12V CPU power connector). Two 4 pin ATX 12V CPU power connectors equal one 8 pin EPS 12V CPU power connector. Therefore, for compatibility with both types of CPU power connectors, many power supplies provide 4+4 pin ATX/EPS 12V CPU connectors (a.k.a., 4+4 pin ATX 12V CPU connectors).
  • Power supply must provide correct CPU power connector(s).
  • PCIe slots provide up to 75W to PCIe cards. Therefore, PCIe cards/add-in GPUs that use less than 75W might have a power supply minimum wattage requirement, and PCIe cards/add-in GPUs that use more than 75W always have a power supply minimum wattage requirement and one or more PCIe power connectors.
  • PCIe power connector: The connection between power supply and PCIe card/add-in GPU to provide additional power to the PCIe card/add-in GPU.
  • PCIe power connector types: 1.) 6 pin (2 rows x 3 pins each) PCIe power connector, which provides up to 75W; and 2.) 8 pin (2 rows x 4 pins each) PCIe power connector, which provides up to 150W. Except for the 2 additional pins, the 6 pin and 8 pin PCIe power connectors are identical. Therefore, for compatibility with both types of PCIe power connectors, many power supplies provide 6+2 pin PCIe power connectors.
  • To install PCIe card/add-in GPU, power supply must provided PCIe card/add-in GPU power supply requirements, if any, including power supply minimum wattage and correct PCIe power connector(s).
  • Peripheral power connector: The connection between power supply and PATA hard drive to power the PATA hard drive.
  • Peripheral power connector type: 4 pin peripheral power connector (a.k.a., 4 pin molex power connector).
  • To install PATA HD, power supply must provide peripheral power connector.
  • SATA power connector: The connection between power supply and SATA hard drive to power the SATA hard drive.
  • SATA power connector type: 15 pin SATA power connector.
  • To install SATA HD, power supply must provide SATA power connector.
  • Floppy disk drive power connector: The connection between power supply and floppy disk drive to power the floppy disk drive.
  • Floppy disk drive power connector type: 4 pin floppy disk drive power connector.
  • To install floppy disk drive, power supply must provide floppy disk drive power connector.
  • Case and power supply design affect computer cooling. Manufacturers of both cases and power supplies should be best positioned and most motivated to develop solutions to computer cooling problems. Therefore, I prefer power supplies from manufactures of both cases and power supplies.
  • Manufacturers of both cases and power supplies include Antec (antec.com), Cooler Master (coolermaster.com), Corsair (corsair.com), Lian Li (lian-li.com), and Thermaltake (thermaltakeusa.com).
  • Look for case/power supply bundle to reduce cost. Be careful, however, that case/power supply bundles - even from name brand manufacturers - often include low quality generic brand (a.k.a., stock) ATX12V v2.0 power supplies, not high quality name brand ATX12V v2.2 power supplies or above.
